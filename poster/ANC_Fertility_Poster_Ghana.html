Temporal and Spatial Dynamics of Antenatal Care Coverage and Fertility Inequities in Ghana:
A Subnational Ecological Study Using Nine DHS Survey Waves (1988–2022)

Valentine Golden Ghanem | Ghana COCOBOD Cocoa Clinic, Accra, Ghana | ORCID: 0009-0002-8332-0220

Background & Rationale

Ghana's national antenatal care (ANC) coverage has improved markedly from 68.0% (1988) to 97.9% (2022), yet subnational inequities persist, masked by national aggregates. The co-occurrence of high fertility (Total Fertility Rate, TFR) and low skilled ANC uptake creates compounding maternal health risk in northern and rural districts.

Part II of a longitudinal series, this study introduces the Care Efficiency Index (CEI = ANC% ÷ TFR) as a composite metric for identifying regions where healthcare access gains are eroded by fertility burden.

Study Design & Data

Design: Repeated cross-sectional ecological study using nine nationally representative DHS survey rounds.

Population unit: 16 administrative regions of Ghana (post-2018 subdivision); earlier waves standardised to 10-region equivalents.

  • Observations: 90 region-year panels
  • Primary outcome: Skilled ANC coverage (%)
  • Primary exposure: Total Fertility Rate (TFR)
  • Novel metric: CEI = ANC% ÷ TFR (range: 14.3–30.8)
  • DHS waves: 1988, 1993, 1998, 2003, 2008, 2014, 2016, 2019, 2022
  • Covariates: Poverty index, female secondary education, uninsurance rate, adolescent fertility rate, rural population %

Statistical Methods

  • Spatial autocorrelation: Global Moran's I (KNN k=8, 999 permutations) + Bivariate LISA (Rook contiguity)
  • Machine learning: Random Forest (ANC prediction) + CART (risk classification); 5-fold spatial CV
  • Partial dependence: Friedman's PDPs for TFR threshold identification
  • Risk stratification: 2×2 z-score quadrant (CEI z-score × TFR z-score)
  • Trend decomposition: Segmented linear regression for ANC temporal trend
  • Inequality: Gini coefficient for between-region ANC disparity
  • Software: Python 3.11 (scikit-learn, libpysal, esda, geopandas); R 4.3 (lme4)
  • Seed: 42 (all stochastic operations)

Key Novel Contribution

The CEI disambiguates where ANC coverage gains are efficiency-adjusted for fertility burden, identifying regions that appear ANC-sufficient but remain high-risk under high TFR. This index provides a programmatically actionable triage lens for maternal health investment.

Discussion

Despite near-universal national ANC coverage by 2022, the CEI reveals a persistent 2.15-fold efficiency gap between Greater Accra (CEI=30.8) and North East (CEI=14.3). The critical TFR threshold of 5.90, identified by Random Forest partial dependence plots, marks a non-linear inflection point beyond which ANC uptake declines sharply — driven by geographic isolation, poverty, and low female secondary education rates.

The spatial clustering of the Critical and Emerging risk zones in Ghana's northern belt (Moran's I=0.61, p<0.001 for TFR) confirms that fertility-driven ANC suppression is not a random phenomenon but a spatially structured equity failure requiring targeted subnational intervention.

The convergence of the ANC Gini coefficient from 0.142 (1988) to 0.038 (2022) signals meaningful national progress, yet masks the absolute burden in Critical zones where adolescent fertility rates remain above 150/1,000 women aged 15–19.

Policy Implications

  • Target Critical zones first: North East, Savannah, Upper West require integrated ANC-family planning packages, not ANC expansion alone.
  • TFR 5.90 as programmatic trigger: Districts exceeding this threshold should trigger automatic resource allocation under Ghana's Reproductive Health Service Policy.
  • CEI as routine DHIMS2 indicator: National Health Insurance Authority and Ghana Health Service should compute CEI quarterly at district level to guide investment decisions.
  • Female education priority: RF feature importance (0.34) confirms secondary education as the single strongest predictor of ANC efficiency — aligned with Ghana Education Service targets.
  • Legal basis: ICESCR Art. 12 (right to health) + Ghana Public Health Act 2012 (Act 851) + Maputo Protocol Art. 14 mandate targeted action in Critical-zone regions.

Limitations

  • Ecological fallacy: regional estimates do not permit individual-level causal inference.
  • Historical DHS waves used 10-region framework; standardisation to 16 regions introduced mapping uncertainty for pre-2018 data.
  • DHIMS2 data unavailable for pre-2012 period; DHS relied for full timeline.
  • Cross-sectional DHS design cannot establish temporal precedence within each wave.
